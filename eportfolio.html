About the Course

Living in the
IT Era

The course Living in the IT Era introduces students to the importance of information and communication technology (ICT) in today's modern society. It discusses how digital technology affects communication, education, business, entertainment, and everyday human interactions.

The course also emphasizes responsible and ethical use of technology by exploring topics such as netiquette, computer ethics, cybercrimes, internet threats, and digital citizenship. Furthermore, students learn about the role of ICT in social change and the opportunities and challenges brought by the digital age.

Through this course, learners are encouraged to become informed, responsible, and productive digital citizens who can adapt to the rapidly changing technological world.

Course Objectives

Equip students with knowledge and skills to navigate the digital environment confidently, ethically, and safely — promoting responsible digital citizenship in a rapidly evolving world.

  • Understand ICT's role in social change
  • Practice netiquette and computer ethics
  • Recognize and respond to cybercrimes
  • Apply design principles for social impact
  • Create effective digital communications

Weekly Lessons

Course Content & Reflections

Six weeks of learning documented with summaries and insights on ICT, ethics, design, and digital citizenship.

Netiquette & Computer Ethics

Week 1 · Lesson 1
Netiquette Computer Ethics Cybercrimes Digital Citizenship
Netiquette and Computer Ethics
This lesson teaches students how to behave responsibly and ethically online. It covers netiquette (the rules of online communication), computer ethics (principles guiding technology use), cybercrimes, and internet threats. Students learn to distinguish between privacy and security, and how to protect themselves in digital environments.
Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics
This lesson introduces the ten fundamental rules that guide ethical computer use. These include respecting others' privacy, avoiding harm, being honest, and ensuring responsible sharing of information. Students understand that ethical use of technology impacts both personal safety and society.
Cybercrimes and Common Forms
Students explore the different types of cybercrimes, such as hacking, phishing, identity theft, and online scams. The lesson emphasizes recognizing illegal online behaviors and understanding the consequences of participating in them.
Internet Threats and Privacy Risks
This lesson focuses on various threats found online, including malware, viruses, and phishing attacks. Students learn the difference between privacy (control over personal information) and security (protection from threats), and strategies to safeguard themselves online.
Responsible Digital Citizenship
Students are guided to understand their role as responsible digital citizens. This includes practicing netiquette, protecting personal information, respecting others, and using technology to benefit themselves and the community.
Summary
This lesson covers netiquette, the set of rules for respectful online communication, and computer ethics, which provide moral guidelines for using technology safely and fairly. Students learn to identify cybercrimes such as hacking, phishing, and identity theft, as well as internet threats like malware, viruses, and scams.
Insights
The lessons in this module focus on promoting responsible and ethical behavior in the digital world. Through exploring the Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics, students understand the importance of honesty, respect, and responsibility when using technology. The module highlights responsible digital citizenship, encouraging students to use technology in ways that benefit both themselves and the community while maintaining safety and ethical standards.

Digital Technology & Social Change

Week 2 · Lesson 2
ICT Social Change SWOT Digital Age
Digital Technology and Social Change
This lesson introduces students to the relationship between digital technology and societal transformation. It explores how ICT influences social progress, development, and everyday life, examining both benefits and challenges of the digital age, including connectivity, technological dependence, and inequality.
Introduction to the Digital Age
Students learn the fundamental concepts of the digital age, including digitalization, ICT, and the evolution of technology in society. The lesson encourages reflection on how digital tools change communication, learning, work, and social interactions, promoting critical thinking about positive and negative effects.
Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats of the Digital Age
This lesson focuses on analyzing the digital era using the SWOT framework. Students identify the strengths (e.g., accessibility of information), weaknesses (e.g., digital dependency), opportunities (e.g., innovation and global connectivity), and threats (e.g., cybercrime, inequality) posed by digital technologies.
Role of ICT in Social Change
Students explore how ICT acts as a catalyst for social transformation, affecting education, business, communication, and governance. The lesson highlights ways technology can promote social progress, create opportunities, and facilitate participation, while addressing challenges like job displacement and social inequality.
Technology and Society Dynamics
This lesson encourages students to understand complex dynamics between technology and human behavior, including ethical, cultural, and social implications. Discussions include the influence of social media, artificial intelligence, and digital communication on relationships, learning, and societal structures.
Insights
This module explores the profound impact of digital technology on society and human life. The lessons highlight both the strengths and opportunities of the digital era — increased access to information, global connectivity, and innovation — as well as weaknesses and threats, including digital dependence, cybercrime, and widening social inequalities. Students are encouraged to critically evaluate the dynamic relationship between technology and society, considering cultural, ethical, and human consequences of digital transformation.

IT Culture & Internet of Things

Week 3 · Lesson 3
IoT Emerging Tech AI 5G
Introduction to IoT
IoT refers to everyday devices connected to the internet that can communicate with each other to make tasks easier and more efficient — smartphones, smart home devices, and wearable gadgets. IoT is applied in both consumer settings (smart homes, fitness tracking) and enterprise settings (manufacturing, agriculture, logistics).
Consumer and Enterprise Applications
IoT helps improve operations in businesses by providing real-time data and better decision-making. For individuals, it supports smart homes, fitness tracking, and automated appliances.
IoT Security and Privacy Issues
IoT devices collect and share personal data that can be at risk if not properly protected. Understanding these security and privacy concerns is crucial for responsible technology use.
Current Trends and Emerging Technologies
Current trends include artificial intelligence, 5G, cloud computing, and automation, which enhance IoT capabilities. Emerging trends such as smart cities, blockchain, virtual reality, and big data are key drivers of future development.
The Innovation Dice
A tool to encourage creativity by combining different ideas to form new solutions — illustrating how technology continues to evolve and become part of everyday life.
Summary
The lesson showed how IoT refers to everyday devices connected to the internet. The discussion covered consumer and enterprise applications, security and privacy risks, and current trends including AI, 5G, cloud computing, and automation. Emerging technologies such as smart cities, blockchain, and big data were discussed as key drivers of future development.
Insights
Technology, especially the Internet of Things (IoT), plays an important role in making everyday life easier and more efficient. It is used not only by individuals but also by businesses to improve processes, save time, and make better decisions. Another important insight is the need to be responsible in using these technologies — while they offer many benefits, they also come with risks such as security and privacy issues.

Imaging & Design for Social Impact

Week 4 · Lesson 4
Graphic Design Branding Typography Color
Topics Covered
This lesson introduces core design principles: Graphic Design Fundamentals · Branding and Identity · Layout and Composition · Typography · Color · Image
Summary
The lesson on Imaging and Design for Social Impact focused on the basic ideas of graphic design and how visuals can be used to communicate messages effectively. Branding and identity were discussed, showing how logos, colors, and styles help represent a group or organization. Layout and composition are important in arranging elements properly so designs look balanced and easy to understand. Typography was explained as a way to make text readable and visually appealing. Colors can express emotions and catch attention, while images help deliver messages quickly and clearly. Overall, the lesson showed that good design is not just about making things look nice, but about communicating ideas and creating a positive impact on society.
Insights
Graphic design is not just about making things look attractive, but about communicating a clear message. Elements like layout, typography, color, and images must work together so that people can easily understand the design. Design can be used as a powerful tool for social impact — through proper use of visuals, designers can raise awareness, influence people's thoughts, and promote positive change. This means that being creative also comes with responsibility.

Photo Imaging & Post-Processing

Week 5 · Lesson 5
Photography Exposure Post-Processing Composition
Topics Covered
History of Photography · Types of Photography · Exposure · Top Ten Tips for Taking Great Pictures · Online Resources
Insights
Photography is more than simply taking pictures — it requires both technical understanding and artistic vision. A good photograph depends not only on expensive equipment but also on the photographer's ability to control light, composition, and camera settings effectively. Understanding concepts such as aperture, shutter speed, and ISO helps photographers intentionally create specific moods and effects rather than relying on automatic settings.

Post-processing is not simply changing a photo but improving and refining the captured image to better communicate the intended message or story. Technology has expanded the possibilities of photography, allowing photographers to correct imperfections and enhance creativity while maintaining image quality. Photography is a skill that develops through practice, creativity, observation, and proper use of digital tools.

Creating Infographics for Social Campaigns

Week 6 · Lesson 6
Infographics Typography Contrast Balance Color
Design Elements for Effective Infographics
Key topics include: Typography · Photography · Contrast · Balance · Color · Consistency · Negative Space
Summary
The lesson on creating infographics for social campaigns explains how infographics serve as powerful visual tools for presenting information, data, and messages in a clear, organized, and engaging way. It discusses the importance of planning an infographic carefully by identifying the story behind the data, creating an outline, writing a compelling title, and selecting an appropriate layout or grid design. The lesson introduces important infographic design elements such as typography, photography, contrast, balance, color, consistency, and negative space. Effective social campaigns use strong visuals, simple yet meaningful content, and audience-centered design to raise awareness and communicate important social issues more effectively.
Insights
Creating an effective infographic requires more than artistic creativity — it also involves proper planning, organization, and understanding of design principles. Elements such as color, typography, balance, and contrast play an important role in guiding the viewer's attention and making information easier to understand. Combining creativity, visual communication skills, and audience awareness makes infographics more impactful, meaningful, and effective in spreading information in the digital age.
